More Than Just Food

Sushi is an art form that balances flavor, texture, and aesthetic beauty. Originating in Southeast Asia as a way to preserve fish, it has evolved into a global culinary phenomenon.

Types of Sushi

Nigiri

Hand-pressed sushi rice topped with a slice of fresh fish.

Maki

Sushi rice and fillings rolled in dried seaweed (nori).

Sashimi

Fresh raw fish or meat sliced into thin pieces (no rice).

Temaki

Hand-rolled cone of seaweed, rice, and fillings.

The Anatomy of Tuna

Akami

Lean, red meat from the back. Firm texture and rich flavor.

Chutoro

Medium fatty, found near the skin on the back and belly. Perfect balance.

Otoro

The fattiest belly cut. Melts in your mouth with intense sweetness.

Popular Fish & Cuts

Salmon (Sake)

Belly (Harasu)

The fattiest and most flavorful part of the salmon. Rich, buttery texture.

Back (Segawa)

Leaner meat with a firm texture and clean taste. Great for nigiri.

Tuna (Maguro)

Akami (Lean)

Deep red, lean meat from the back. Classic tuna flavor.

Chutoro (Medium Fatty)

Perfect balance of fat and meat. Found on the side/back.

Otoro (Fatty Belly)

The prized belly cut. Intensely marbled and melts in the mouth.

Yellowtail (Hamachi)

Loin

Firm, white-pink flesh with a sweet, rich flavor.

Belly

Higher fat content, softer texture and very savory.

History

4th Century BC

Narezushi: Fermented fish with rice (rice was discarded).

Edo Period (1800s)

Hanaya Yohei invents Edomae sushi (generic Nigiri), using fresh fish for quick eating.

Modern Era

Sushi spreads globally, evolving with new ingredients like salmon and avocado.

Etiquette

Do

Eat nigiri with your hands or chopsticks.

Don't

Mix wasabi into your soy sauce (for high-end sushi).

Do

Dip only the fish side into the soy sauce, not the rice.
